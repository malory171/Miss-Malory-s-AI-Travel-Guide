Country Overview

French Polynesia is an overseas collectivity of France, located in the South Pacific Ocean. It consists of 118 islands scattered over an area as large as Europe, but only 67 islands are inhabited. These islands are grouped into five main archipelagos: the Society Islands (which include Tahiti and Bora Bora), the Tuamotu Archipelago, the Marquesas Islands, the Gambier Islands, and the Austral Islands. Known for its dramatic landscapes, French Polynesia boasts lush mountains, crystal-clear lagoons, and coral reefs, making it a favorite destination for those seeking natural beauty and relaxation.

Population

As of 2023, French Polynesia has a population of about 280,000 people, with more than two-thirds of the population living on the island of Tahiti, home to the capital city, Papeete. The population is predominantly Polynesian, but there are also French and Chinese communities, contributing to the cultural diversity of the region.

Languages Spoken

The official language of French Polynesia is French, used in government, education, and media. However, Tahitian (Reo Tahiti) is widely spoken and is a key element of cultural identity for the indigenous Polynesians. Many inhabitants also speak English, especially in tourist-heavy areas, as well as other Polynesian dialects depending on the island group.

Famous Places to Visit

  • Bora Bora: Known worldwide for its luxurious overwater bungalows, turquoise lagoons, and white-sand beaches, Bora Bora is often described as the "Pearl of the Pacific." The island is a favorite among honeymooners and adventure seekers alike, offering activities such as snorkeling, diving, and shark feeding.
  • Tahiti: The largest island and the heart of French Polynesia, Tahiti is home to the capital, Papeete. It offers a mix of natural beauty and modern amenities, including black-sand beaches, tropical waterfalls like Fautaua Falls, and a bustling market (March√© de Papeete) where visitors can purchase local crafts, fresh produce, and Tahitian pearls.
  • Moorea: Just a short ferry ride from Tahiti, Moorea is famous for its rugged, volcanic landscape, clear blue waters, and dense tropical vegetation. The island is a popular spot for snorkeling, hiking, and experiencing traditional Polynesian culture.
  • Rangiroa: Part of the Tuamotu Archipelago, Rangiroa is one of the largest atolls in the world. It is a diving paradise with coral reefs teeming with marine life, including dolphins, manta rays, and sharks. The Pink Sands Beach and the Blue Lagoon are other must-see attractions on this island.
  • Fakarava: A UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, Fakarava is one of the best-preserved coral atolls in the world. It is ideal for divers, offering crystal-clear waters and vibrant underwater ecosystems filled with colorful fish, reef sharks, and pristine coral gardens.
  • The Marquesas Islands: Remote and rugged, the Marquesas Islands offer a stark contrast to the lagoon-filled islands of the Society group. These islands are known for their steep cliffs, deep valleys, and ancient archaeological sites. They are a must-visit for adventure travelers seeking authentic Polynesian culture and untamed nature.
